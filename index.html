Sheffield's Homeless Problem

Should we give beggars money?

Homelessness is a topic that divides us; and whilst levels of homelessness increase with austerity, opinions of the homeless haven’t much changed.

As a nation, many feel that people are homeless because they’ve taken the wrong roads in life, that they’ve made the wrong decisions; ultimately, that it’s their fault.

According to The Poverty Page, the most common cause of homelessness is a loss of accommodation provided by friends or relatives. Homeless people, as experts are keen to articulate, are not just those walking around with four cans of special brew.

Walking through Sheffield, you encounter homeless people of all shapes, sizes and genders. There are migrant women, young people, ex-military men and old agers. Homelessness doesn’t discriminate. As Graham Bostock, community leader at Sheffield Emmaus, said: “We’re all just a little step away from homelessness.”

One question, perhaps the question that divides us most of all, is whether we should give beggars money. Some choose not to, believing it contributes to a cycle of street sleeping and addiction. Some hand over what change they have in their pockets. Others bridge the gap, buying the beggar a sandwich, a chocolate bar, a bottle of water.

We spoke to three separate organisations working with the homeless. They told us what work they do and whether or not we should give money to beggars.

Emmaus

A Place to Feel Safe and Secure

A Tale of Companionship

Emmaus was set up by after World War 2 by Abbé Pierr, a Catholic priest, MP and resistance fighter.

Abbe Pierre was approached one night by a man named Georges, who after being released from a 20 year prison sentence, had tried to commit suicide. He turned to the Abbe for help, who in turn asked Georges for help in building houses for homeless mothers.

Emmaus is now worldwide, and has over 20 communities in England alone.

Sheffield’s Emmaus, which started up in 2007, is situated near Sheffield City Centre’s canal basin, a five minute walk from the town.

Graham Bostock, community leader at Emmaus Sheffield, said: “The whole ethos of Emmaus is that the homeless come to live here. They sign off any primary benefits and they live and work 40 hours in an ordinary working environment.”

Sheffield City Council

The Key is Preservation

Bucking the Trend

One problem is getting this person to engage with us and the other services.

“Another issue comes with funding. When you’re dealing with vulnerable and homeless people, there’s never enough money and you can always do more.

“Something else you might find interesting, that we have to deal with every day since social media began. We receive phone calls from people who have seen a social media campaign; there’ll have been a post shared and viewed thousands of times, saying that a certain homeless person isn’t being helped in any way.

“Actually, there’s a lot of work gone into that person, and they just don’t want to move on. Dealing with those phone calls take up a lot of time we could be using more positively in actually helping people.”

Cathedral Archer Project

Giving the Homeless Opportunity and Choices

Open Doors

The Cathedral Archer Project has its roots in the unemployment of the 1980s.

After a decade of decline in Sheffield’s industry, many people found themselves in the grips of poverty and ill health.

By 1989, Sheffield Cathedral had become a haven for those with nowhere else to turn. They were given a breakfast by the congregation, with Church Army Captain Alan Turner overseeing the affair. This was the beginnings of the Cathedral Archer Project.

“I think Sheffield are doing okay with the homelessness problem.” - Andy Parfrement

In 2007, the Project moved to its own premises at the back of the Cathedral, fitted with a kitchen, a lounge, showers and laundry services, a medical room and an education room.

Andy Parfrement, manager at the Project, said: “We provide basic support in terms of shower facilities, food, literacy and numeracy lessons, help with job searches, leisure activities such as photography, football, comic making and art groups.

“We also have an onsite nurse, dentist and chiropodist, an advice worker that comes from the councils housing and advice service; all the different things a homeless person might need.”

“I think Sheffield are doing okay with the homelessness problem. Generally we have quite a low homeless population, less than London and Manchester. The services are here if any homeless person needs them, and if the homeless present to housing solutions, they will be accommodated.”

One major tool in the battle against homelessness is the connection between the homeless services of Sheffield; as Zoe Young said, “we deal with services like Turning Point and the Cathedral Archer Project every hour of every day.”

Turning Point - an organisation dealing with homelessness, addiction, learning difficulties, and mental illness - actually has its Sheffield branch at the back of the Cathedral, on the same site as the Project.

“One of the good things about being so connected with the other services is there’s now a faster route into treatment for addiction than there was ten years ago. It only takes a few weeks to get into treatment now, where as it was three to six months in times gone by.

“One thing that we can’t deal with though, and a lot of it is to do with national policy, is migrants who are homeless. They don’t have passports or formal documents, so we can only feed them and send them away. We can’t put them in housing. We can’t help them with job searches” said Parfrement.

After twenty five years of service to the homeless community, the Project is still going strong, and has gained a reputation as one of Sheffield’s most successful organisations in the battle against homelessness, giving the vulnerable the basic support they need whilst referring them to services who can offer longer-lasting solutions.

“All in all, I think what we try to do at project is give people opportunity and choices, and people will walk through the doors when they’re ready” said Parfrement.
